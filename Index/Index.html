Reading 2: the Poetry of Tools

1. In the opening paragraph of her post, Seu asks you, the reader to join her to 'defamiliarize our tools.' What do you think she means by that? And how do you think that action relates to her use of the word poetry?

By asking the reader to join her to 'defamiliarize our tools', she means that people need to think deeper on notion of interaction with digital technology so that we understand it in a more conscious and meaningful way. She relates her point to the use of the word poetry by first giving the reader a quote from David Jhave Johnston. She thinks that the relationship between "poem" and "poets" in digital aspect has been obscured by the poetic tools from digital technology. She also points out that people need to consider that technology more as a form of language rather than only as a tool so that it can help us recognize its poetic potential.

2. Seu outlines 6 categories within her taxonomy of tool poetics (Slow Growth & Slow Death, Early Works, Performing Data, Removing Function from Form, Production of Errors, & Regeneration). Choose at least one of these categories and define it in your own words. Now find a website or online interface* not mentioned in her post that you feel operates within the chosen definition. Explain why.

Reading 1: Touring Complete User

1. Lialina contradicts her idea to Norman by arguing that the word “user” still needs to be considered not “people”. For example, she supposes that naming as people instead of users hides the existence of two classes of people; developers and users. She even points out that users may lose their rights and the opportunity to protect them when not being called users. She thinks the word “user” is very crucial for perceiving and designing what users need and therefore having a better system for the users. She states that meaning of to be user should be better understood.

2. Lialina’s ideal internet user is “general purpose users” who are not professional at programming and understanding computers – who are busy with something else. She outlines the Universal user and explains that “there can be different examples and levels that users can imagine for themselves, but the capacity to be universal is still in all of us”. She means that the general purpose users interact with the app or online system by their own way, for their own purposes such as using Live Journal as if it is Twitter. She stresses out that the general purpose users are the one who are the ultimate participants of “man-computer symbiosis”.

3. One example from my experience where there were “gaps that can be filled by users”, was with buffering when watching an entertainment program. There was once this video uploaded on Youtube and had too much buffering which was wasting my time. To actually complete watching all the program, I opened a new tab, replayed the same video, and paused to the estimated time where there would be the buffering for the first video I was watching. When the buffering occurred, I went to that second tab and finished watching the entertainment program.

Theorizing the Web 2018

April 27 - 28

Museum of the Moving Image

in Astoria, Queens

Theorizing the Web is an inter- and non-disciplinary annual conference that brings together scholars, journalists, artists, activists, and technology practitioners to think conceptually and critically about the interrelationships between the Web and society. We deeply value public engagement, and consider insights from academics, non-academics, and non-“tech theorists” alike to be equally valuable.

Morning Program

Friday April, 27
10:30AM Doors Open
Noon–1:15pm Session 1
Studio A #A1 Body Politics Studio B #B1 Tech Support Studio C #C1 May Quest
From intimate depictions of bodies to substantial changes to the body itself, digitality permeates us. To sustain protest we must also turn to one another for support. Can digital media serve as a means for this without undermining it? Digitality hasn't obviated geography as was anticipated; it has made geopolitical boundaries simultaneously more visible and more intensely monitored. Sometimes technology even makes visible border practices that states intend to be invisible.
Moderator: Whitney Erin Boesel Moderator: Michael Connor Moderator: Jeremy Antley
